Signs You Need an Electrical Panel Upgrade

How to tell when your electrical service is falling behind your home's needs.

Electrical panels are designed to last decades, but most homes eventually reach a point where the original panel can no longer keep up. Whether it's an older service size, a full breaker box, or equipment that's showing its age, the signs below can help you decide whether it's time for an evaluation.

Breakers That Trip Repeatedly

Occasional trips are normal, but frequent tripping on the same circuit usually means the load has grown beyond what the circuit was designed to handle. Before resetting and moving on, consider whether new appliances, an EV charger, or added square footage have pushed the load past its limit.

No Space for New Circuits

If your panel has no open breaker slots, adding a new circuit for a workshop, EV charger, or home addition requires either a subpanel or a full panel replacement. A licensed electrician can evaluate your options and recommend the most cost-effective path.

Your Panel Is 25–40 Years Old

Older panels—especially those with known brands like Federal Pacific (Stab-Lok) or Zinsco—have a documented history of safety issues. Even panels without those red flags may no longer meet current load demands. Age alone is a reason to have the panel inspected.

Visible Rust, Scorch Marks, or Burning Smell

Any signs of corrosion, heat damage, or a persistent burning odor near your panel are serious. These can indicate a loose connection, overloaded bus, or failing breaker. Shut off the affected circuits if safe to do so and call an electrician.

Lights Flicker When Large Appliances Start

Momentary dimming when a large appliance starts can signal a service that's running close to its limits. If flickering is consistent or affects multiple rooms, it's worth having the overall load evaluated.

You're Planning a Major Addition or Upgrade

Adding a new HVAC system, EV charger, induction range, hot tub, or workshop often requires more capacity than older panels were sized for. A service upgrade at the beginning of a renovation is much easier than retrofitting it later.

What a Panel Upgrade Typically Involves

A service upgrade generally includes replacing the main panel, upgrading the service entrance wiring, coordinating with the utility for any meter work, and labeling all circuits clearly. Scope varies based on the existing service size, the target amperage, and whether the meter or entrance cable also need attention. Learn more on our panel upgrades & service upgrades page.

When to Call for a Panel Inspection

If you're unsure whether an upgrade is needed, start with an inspection. An electrician can review your current load, identify any safety concerns, and give you a clear picture of your options before you commit to a project.
